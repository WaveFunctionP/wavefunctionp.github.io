Speed Of Gravity Measured...Surprise!, Einstein Was Right

Hey, what news! Einstein was right. Scientists have measured the speed of gravity using the movement of Jupiter into the lightpath of a quasar. By knowing the mass of Jupiter, corrections were taken into account for the bending of light that should occur and observing the movement of light as Jupiter passed in front of the quasar. As Jupiter's gravity bent the light as it passed, measurements were made to check the speed that the gravity influenced the light passing nearby. Final numbers came to about 95% of the speed of light with an error of 25%, so it was fairly accurate. This confirms Einstein's prediction in General Relativity that gravity propagates at the same rate as light and further fueling the speculation that gravity is akin to light in a very fundamental way. Hello, supersymmetry.

I wish I knew what they were talking about when the say it disproves many newer theories the involve many dimensions. Superstring theory, in all it's various flavors, is probably included on that list. Supposedly, gravity could "shortcut" it's way through space utilizing these extra dimensions, but I assume it's just a pretty mathematics. Some kind of numerical voodoo that had nothing to do with physical observations. But such is the case without declining budgets given to physics departments nowadays. Only time will tell what effect that will have on economic development here in the US. Enough ranting, this is a great development.

Nuclear Batteries

So far most of the nanotech articles reported on here have relied on glucose in the blood stream to power most of the devices proposed for medical uses. Well, just what happens if you need to use the same technology outside the body or in a fashion that doesn't allow a piggyback ride so to speak. Most ideas center around batteries, but that presents a problem in it's self. Common batteries we use today don't scale that well down to the size we need and still provide adequate power. Enter nuclear batteries. These proposed power sources, although dubious in name, could provide a viable solution. It uses really weak radioactive isotopes. So weak in fact that after just a couple of half lives the emitted radiation is lower than that of concrete. It uses a level to collect charges as particle are emitted. As the charges build up it create an attraction that between that of the level and base creating movement that powers a servo that acts as a generator converting the attraction movement into useable electricity. When the charges build up enough, the level move into contact with the base and the charges are equalled resetting the lever and starting the cycle all over again. I'm still not sure why they don't just use the charge difference it's self as a battery, but I'm sure I've gotten my understanding of physics somehow. Anywho, it works and now they are working to scale it down to ever smaller sizes so it can be used in future MEMS.

A Level-headed Look at Microsoft

Microsoft has been through a lot during the past few years. Legal battles, questionable security initiatives (Palladium), and confusing software platforms (.Net) have all served to drag the company to new lows. Many claim the eventual demise of the juggernaut and still otherwise. What's going on at Redmond? Kuro5hin has an interview with Adam Barr, a former Microsoft developer who seems to have levelheaded view at to what has happened. It's hard to get truth when it comes to Microsoft simple because most of the most outspoken are either adamantly against or for the software giant.

Many people consider Microsoft to be this all-powerful entity that always gets its way and succeeds in whatever it attempts (whether through technical ability, luck, marketing, or illegal means). But in fact, if you look back at all the initiatives Microsoft has attempted, most of them have failed. Remember Windows at Work, an attempt to unify all office machines on a network? Or TrueImage, a clone of the PostScript page description language? Or ACE, the Advanced Computing Environment, designed to replace the Intel PC architecture? If you look at all the things that Microsoft has boldly announced with a gaggle of industry partners, the success rate is maybe 10 to 20 percent. So pick any ten things that Microsoft is attempting now: Auto PC, cell phone operating system, set-top box, Xbox, electronic books, Internet Gaming Zone, Tablet PC, Pocket PC, speech recognition, MSN. The odds are that only one or two of those will actually succeed.
The last time I read anything that wasn't biased with ridicule or praise was World War 3.0 by Ken Auletta. The book was written before the antitrust trails were over and explains the mentality going on at Microsoft and enlightens the viewer on some of the details of the battle. It's a good read for any who want to know more than is put out by the media and open-source community.

My view: Simply put, I believe that Microsoft is a vicious competitor. Do I believe that the actions it has taken to compete are unfair? Yes, but I don't think that it came from specific management strategies, but more from a mentality to compete. From what I read Microsoft breeds a brutal competitive atmosphere and allows too much independence to it's various departments. Overly zealous employees work hard to compete with a small guy fights giant mentality and hasn't quite gotten the picture that's they're no longer the small guy. Perhaps Microsoft is just too good a competitor and needs to let it's enemies catch up. Microsoft needs to realize that, even though it is a very unstable market were a good idea can overtake a giant's, it still can't afford to act like it's playing catch up and act like the leader it is.

Yes, I still want to believe in Microsoft.

P.S. I have to agree with Mr. Barr, Linux is still too much tech for the average Joe. Linux on the desktop will never see widespread use until developers start working to a better experience. It can't be just as good, it has to be a better experience for users to switch.(costs aside)
