AmphetaDesk SideBar

I've been trying in vein to find a adequate news program for windows. There are a few on the market, but few utilize a 3 pane design with a simple interface. Well, that is 'till I found AmphetaDesk. I've used it before, but because it didn't use the requisite 3 window panes, I left in alone to find another solution. I did like AmphetaDesk for it's simplicity and light weight. After a few weeks of using NewzCrawler, I slowly began to dislike many of is quirks and especially that I was going to have to pay for it come a few days. It really didn't justify the cost in my mind.

Fast Forward to yesterday. I reloaded AmphetaDesk and found out you could customize the templates used to generate the headlines page. I've also been hearing a lot of noise on the net about Mozilla sidebars. Couple that with a little JavaScript and viola, 3 pane (sorta) RSS newsreader mozilla sidebar. [screenshot] It's a bad pic, but you get the idea.

The core idea is to use AmphetaDesk to aggregate the feeds, along with it's feed management features, modify the index template to fit in a sidebar and add some collapsible menus to the feed list. Tweak and you've got a totally free, easy to use, way to read news feeds.

All you need to get it running is to download and install AmphetaDesk, copy this index file to your template folder(right click, save target as, rename to index.html), and install the side bar. (The last link is a javascript that installs the AmphetaDesk URI: "http://127.0.0.1:8888/index.html", to your sidebar)

Note. I've removed the excerpt/description container from the perl script because the list was too long for my tastes. If you want it just copy the appropriate container from the default template to the new one.

Remember to back up your default template (rename it, or copy it to another directory) before you over write it with the new template. It really doesn't matter, because you should have the zip file you downloaded that you can restore from, but it might save you some trouble if you've already made some changes to the template that you want to save.

All page formatting is done in DOM, so in other words the sidebar is just a webpage. CSS and scripting all apply the same as if it were in the main window. So, CSS is how I chose to apply style. target="_content" in the "a" tags makes the links display in the main window instead of the side bar. Sidebar information can be found here and an AmphetaDesk template explanation is here. (Don't know perl? Neither do I, all you need to know is all variables start with "$", other than that you'll catch on pretty quick)

Enough of this noise, have fun. Remember to tell me what you think, any suggestions?

The Evolution of Online Media and Democracy

The growth of the internet has had an enormous impact on social collaboration. It's seems as if we've only touched the surface. What lies ahead, although unknown, is at once interesting, treacherous, and definitley revolutionary. Here I explore a few ideas on what may come. Just a few of the more surprising results of a pervasive and obiquitous web community. One that transends the computer on which is was built. The very nature and mentality on society at large may see a significant shift in thinking.

I still find it intriguing to speculate over how the internet will change the way we access information and art. The net has been going for some time, but I think we've yet to harness the full power that open collaboration has to offer. Many people still choose to just read a site's content despite the authors effort to provide and open forum for discussion; even *gasp* here at MCTPhysics. I've had a growing suspicion that this current debate over intellectual property issues will take a more optimistic turn, but I don't think things have gotten as bad as they are going to get. Major IP owners and distributors have yet to use up their substantial power base and I fear that the battle will be long and hard. Only until we've reached the breaking point with Mom and Pop realizing that there favorite media can no longer be used in a fair and reasonable way. They will see that it's a result of heavy handed politics and regulation by their favorite Senator or Congressman with substantial campaign contributions from said entities. I see that as the crucial turning point, and I think we've a way to go before that happens.

It doesn't stop there though and I'm not sure if sure which will happen first. The next revolution will be collaboration on a whole new level. Many people working on one piece of artwork or music. Books, journals and news papers being published by people by people who've never even met? This already happens on a significant scale already, but I think we've just touched the surface. Government and organizations that are run totally online, but have appreciable influence offline. This is really interesting. Perhaps in the distant future we will have an America that is run totally online with each interest area (economics, foreign policy, local zoning regulations, education) all run, not by a bureaucracy of disinterested political types, but by expert committees and open discussion forums accessible by anyone interested. This is the convergence that I see as the emergence of true democracy, where everyone has a voice and is free to speak it in a proportional manner, and have the same power to change as the next guy. No more will people have to concern themselves with topic that they don't understand if they don't want to, and everyone can be assured of legislation on issues by a true consensus of individuals in their area of expertise.

At first site this seems site this seems preposterous. Perhaps this is a bit utopian, but if you can believe with me that we do not have a democracy here in America, but some sort of media giant monarchy. Then maybe you can see a need for something new. Maybe this is a back to basics philosophy to go back and revisit the logistical limitations that the Forefathers had when they drafted the Constitution. This won't come about over night, and presumable nowhere in my lifetime, but maybe my children will have the benefit of a more open and socially reflective government. It'll happen in phases. If you listen closely, you can hear the first whispers. The U.S. government is standardizing it's online efforts and you can see the beginnings of a collaborative effort in the mandatory forums and feedback systems planned.

This plan goes beyond any system of online voting, although this is encompassed in its whole. The details are vague and many problems are unforeseeable. Who knows if whether or not our expert system will not be slighted to the views of the population of that interest sector or if it will still reflect and the general opinion of the population at large, given adequate understanding of the issues involved. This system in engendered off the belief that people tend to be compassionate and understanding and appeals directly to our social nature. When dealing with their fellow man., and given the opportunity to see outside themselves to consider the impact of their decisions as an impact on society at large. Not just another insignificant ballot in the box for some politician that they could care less about; never mind not even caring about. In other words, this will depend solely on appealing to the higher values of the human conscience. It's a lofty goal, but after 9/11, one has a hard time deigning that the American heart isn't dead.

Maybe one day the president will be nothing more than a figure head similar to Britain's Queen. Again all this is speculation, but I'd really like to here your feedback on the comments board.

Using MoveableType with DHTML Javascript

As if you didn't know, MoveableType is the BOMB! Well, OK, maybe not that good. But it's what I use for my blog engine and it makes things much easier. As with most blog software that uses the BloogerAPI; it uses a template system to generate the webpage and manage content. Allowing you to focus on your sites news and articles instead of formatting each and every page. MoveableTypes system uses a proprietary system a little different than most. I like to think that it's a little bit easier to use and more flexible. The only problem is getting to know tag system and how it works. Using this system is really easy once you know the lingo, but how do you use the DHTML script to get that ubercool navigation bar?

Here it is in all it's glory.


<MTCategories>

<div id="cat<$MTCategoryID$>">

<div class="sidetitle">

<a href="javascript:catList('cat<$MTCategoryID$>')">[+]</a> <a href="<MTCategoryArchiveLink>"><$MTCategoryLabel$></a>

</div>

<div class="catlist">

<div class="side">

<MTEntries lastn="5">

<a href="<$MTEntryLink$>"><$MTEntryTitle$></a><br />

</MTEntries>

</div>

</div>

</div>

</MTCategories>


Lovely isn't it. It probably provides a little more perspective on why I wrote the script the way I did. Notice the catlist class selector. That selector is the one that gets created in memory when the script loads. It doesn't reside in my CSS file. "Side" and "Sidetitle" are selectors in my CSS file. They actually control the style even when the script isn't running, provided your browser supports CSS. The granularity built in to this sytem makes for a very general way to control the visibilty of you sheet without recoding everytime you add or delete categories or otherwise change your navbar. Also of note is the way "catlist" holds the same value of the javascript perimeter allowing the script to find the correct "catlist" to hide or show.

Nevertheless, this will display all your categories you have in your blog along with the last 5 entries in each. Well, that's about it for MT tags. Enjoy your new navigation bar.

DHTML via Javascript - A Collapsable Text Menu Tutorial

I've been trying to provide as much functionality to this site without making things too complicated for the user. As you can see the navigation bar on the main page has been updated. Clicking the "[+]" will expand the category now. I didn't like the long list of recent articles running down the page, but I wanted to provide a way to get a quick look at the various topics. Let me know what you think.

Now you know what I've been doing for the past couple of days. That's right, I learned to program Javascript. I've never written a program before three days ago. It was hell, but I fully intend to make your's no where as hard as it was for me. Given the plethora of programming centric sites out there, you would think that it would be a simple deal to learn. At the very least, you should be able to get a few clear examples. And in fact you can, if you want to create useless programs that do nothing. The problem is that most sites either assume that you already now how to program in another language or that you know nothing about it. The latter being the worst because they want to walk you through that process step by step while you write junk programs meanwhile totally skirting the real concepts that you need to know.

We won't do that here. If you're interested we'll delve right in.

Writing a program is all about moving and changing objects. it's really simple and frustrating at the same time once you realize that fact. Other places you will read about object oriented programming and how it's like "the way" to program. Well yeah it is, but for a beginner, you will mostly need to concern your self with much lower level objects. I won't do the standard "what is an object" talk, but I will tell you it's all about the API. That is Application Programming Interface. I'll be doing most of the grunt work here and indeed most every program nowadays.

For clarification, we'll only be talking about Javascript this time around. Why Javascript? Because it the easiest lang. that I've found yet. (And the most widespread for its use in DHTML and other web technologies) The most important thing you will need to know before you can begin is a few resources:

Netscape DevEdge - Good reference and documentation site.
W3C DOM - The W3C's Document Object Model Specification. It explains all the types and inheritance and interface properties.

You have to do lots of research to understand what each of the DHTM interfaces do and how to use them. This is crucial to understanding what you are moving around inside the containers. But with out further ado, here is the full script used to control the navigation bar.


function catList(id){
a="+";
b="-";
i=document.getElementById(id);
dval=i.getElementsByTagName("div").item(1).style.display;
t=i.getElementsByTagName("div").item(0).getElementsByTagName("a").item(0).firstChild;
if (dval=="block") {
dval="none";
t.nodeValue=t.nodeValue.replace(b,a);
}
else {
dval="block";
t.nodeValue=t.nodeValue.replace(a,b);
}
i.getElementsByTagName("div").item(1).style.display=dval;
}
if (document.getElementById && document.createTextNode){
document.write('<style type="text/css">.catlist {display:none;}</style>')
}

Purdy ain't it! I still can't believe I spent about 40 hours on this alone. Good thing is, it's reusable otherwise I would have to shoot myself.

I only used W3C DOM specification interfaces to the script should work with any DOM2 browser. IE 5.5 and Netscape 4 qualify for most funtions and Mozilla is fuly qualified. Note:My implementation does not qualify as XHTML. I've used the tag to insert the Category name to provide a unique id for each instance. It doesn't qualify because the names have spaces and CSS id specification doesn't allow spaces in the id. I'll have to find a work around, but this will do for now. You can alternatively use a manual naming format. UPDATE: Although at this time the validation site is down, it should now validate. Dummy me, I forgot about the tag. It's numeric and provided the unique identifier that was needed. I'll post the code in a separate article. It's pretty nifty.

Let the picking begin; starting with the top:


function catList(id){

"function" - we use this to establish a command to call from the web page, i.e. from a link or button.
"catList" - This is the name of the function, I could have named it catNip for that matter.
"(id)" - This is part of the "catList" function. "id" is a variable used to transfer a value to an internal function. (See below) I use this to specify the collection of tags that I want to modify. On the navigation bar it's the category name.
Of course this is were those other tutorials come in handy, syntax. We need a bracket "{" to contain the function. Otherwise you get *gasp* a syntax error. (Whatever shall we do?)

a="+";
b="-";

"a" - is a variable the equal (=) sign assigns the right hand value to the left hand side. Notice I wrote that before I used it in an expression later. Variables must be declared before they are used. "+" is going to be one of the characters is replace via a later expression.
"b" - the other value that I replace with "a".

i=document.getElementById(id);

"i" - another variable that I use to transfer the contents to another container.
"document" - Hey, it one of the those interfaces in our API defined by the DOM. This container performs a generic operation. It finds and returns the current page in the browser window as an object. We use any function or parameter that can be access by the "document" interface. (See what I mean by research. Check out the W3C site for more information.)
"getElementById" - Finds and returns all tags (elements) with the #id matching our parameter "id". No quotes in () because this is a variable being specified. This one was specified earlier in the "function" parameter. You can use any text value for the function which is not always true as we sill see later.

Ars has a review up about the IMAK Smart Glove, a product to help protect your wrist from further injury while allowing them to heal.

The most effective treatment is simple to not rest your elbow on anything to relieve the pressure and inflamation. Along with freaquent breaks, many see full recovery.

After about 3 weeks my pain went away and, after about 3 months, I recieved full feeling back to my hand. There is relief to these problems, but it requires a little change to your routine. The change was well worth it though. Don't forget to talk to your doctor about it.

Quantum Computing Primer

You didn't think I missed all the fuss being made on the news about the recent developments in quantum computers did you? No, I just thought that the subject deserved a bit more than the usual go here read this link up. Although most sites did a good job of getting the word out, most didn't explain some the questions that many readers had about the field in general.

So what, praytell, is all the fuss about? What is a "quantum" computer? What in 'tarnation is decoherence? How does that involve me? Why should I be excited.

A quantum computer (QC) is a computer that operates on the principles and "weirdness" of quantum physics. Quantum processes are very strange in that they any given "particle" that interacts with another exists in a "superposition" of states until measured. Sort of like, and this is stretching it, say someone knocks on your door at your home and you go to answer it. Well, until you open the door and the person sees you; you (to the person that knocked on the door) are in a "superposition" of states: "home" or "not home". Only until you open the door or otherwise give indication that you are indeed "home" or not, you are BOTH "home" and "not home" and NOT "home" and "not home". (yes, you read that right) You will exist in this "superposition" until the knocker observes you (or not) by opening the door. When you are (or, again, not) observed it is known as a "collapse of the wave function" ( It's long story, but I'll cover it in another article.) Ludicrous, huh! Well that's a very vague and liberal explanation. But like it or not, that's quantum mechanics (QM). So, on with the story.

It was Richard P. Feynman who was one of the first to realize that this "superposition" stuff could be very powerful in solving very complex problems where many separate variables were involved. Such as the directed Hamiltonian Path (HP) problem otherwise known as "the traveling salesman" problem. The problem involves a salesman traveling through a number of cities and only visiting them once. The problem is very easy to begin with until the number of cities he has to visit goes up. Until the problem becomes so complex that it is impractical to solve with even the fastest super computers. The problem is complex because it involves so many variables and only one solution. The only way to find out which path to take is to use "trial and error". And miscalculation along the way will lead the solution "down the wrong path" so to speak and you must start all the way over from the beginning and try another route.

Why does a QC have an advantage. Lets go back to that "superposition" mumbo jumbo. QM says that any "particle" (electron, photon, etc.) that interacts with another can become "entangled" with each other. Entanglement means that when something happens to one particle it's entangle partner undergoes the same interaction. No matter how far apart they are. This is interesting because a QC designer could use this to perform many calculations at once. With each calculation knowing what the other is doing.

That's all fine and well, but just how does a designer even make a QC, even in principle. First, he needs a building block. In traditional computers that building block is the transistor with that the designer can represent 1's and 0's as is convention on modern computer systems today. The QC equivalent of this would be the "qubit". With it we will build our computer in a fashion conceptually similar to convetional computers. The major differences would be manufacturing techniques used and programming methods.

Herein lies the signifigance of the latest developements. Most promenent is that of the difficulties of building the physical device. The UNSW device spearheaded by Richard Clark was based on a design from back in 1998 by the mastermind Bruce Cane of the University of Maryland. Whereby two phosphorus atoms were embedded in a silicon crystal. Phosphorous atom were used because they have a slightly better fortitude when it comes to "decoherence", wereby the atom stop behaving in the way need to have the quantum effect. The reason that this development is such good news is that it has proven feasable the ability to build a quantum mechanical device on a silicon substrate. Similar to conventional computer chip manufaturing techniques. Which greatly increases the ease to which it is integrated with mainstream computing. The task was difficult because of the extremely small distances involved, because the phosphorus atoms had to be placed ~20nm from each other. For comparison standard chip manufacturers are using a 130nm process and that's considered the bleeding edge. The researchers actually acheived ~4nm! So, needless to say, the process they used was very ingenious.

But, back to our computer. Say I enter a HP problem into our hypothetical QC. When the computer calculates the problem it first "entangles" as many qubits as is needed and instead of solving each route one by one. It solves them all at once! So now we have a whole array of qubits one of which holds our correct answer and just how do we go about finding it. Well, one of our more observative readers might say something like; "But doesn't reading one of the qbits collapse the wave function and if we read the wrong one we just lost the information". Yes and no, It does collapse the wave function, but since all the qubits are entangled when we read wrong (or right for that matter) qubit we don't get the solution to that single qubit's calculation. "How's that?", you say. Simple, when we read the result, because all the qubits are entengled, all the qubits' wave functions collapse and we get the average solution that the WHOLE system came to, as per QM. Hmm, just what are we going to do with this average when we only need the correct solution of one tiny insignificant qubit? Hehe...The kicker is that the single correct qubit's solution and the entire system average's solution are one and the same. So, instead of having to do multiple operations to find one solution, we did one operation to find many solutions, but the solutions are equivalent. And QM tells us that the larger the system (the more qubits involved) the more accurate it is. Behold the power of QC.

So now we come to our last little piece of news. The significance of which is that this is the first time anyone has made a computation using quantum techniques on a systems proven to be in a quantum state. (that's a mouthful) The computation was carried out by Innsbruck University and involved an operation to decided whether or not a coin was the same or different on each side. It sounds simple (and it is), but the calculations were carried out on both sides at once, utilizing quantum techniques to complete the calculation twice as fast as a conventional computer calculation; again proving the feasability and power of QC.

On another note not directly related (and a bit old), researchers have observed entangled photons remaining entangled even after passing through a barrier, interacting with another object, something not previously thought possible. Because it was thought that interaction with other objects caused the englement effect to disperse. This proves that the effect is much more resilient than previously thought. It could and probably will have tremendous implications on encryption and communication technologies, given further experimentation. I still think this holds the key to faster than light communication, but I'm probably just ignorant.

DoE and Artifcial sight team up

The Department of Energy has commited to contibuting $3 million over 3 years to artificial retina research.

An artificial retina works by implanting an electrode array on the back of the eye to stimulate the nerves that carry the signals the brain where it is processed to produce the images we see everyday. This has been done before in limited form, usually consisting only a few (~16) electrodes, to allow a patient with retinal disease to see the presence or absence of light. Plans to use more (~1000) electrodes will allow a person to see images; albiet in a very limited form.
