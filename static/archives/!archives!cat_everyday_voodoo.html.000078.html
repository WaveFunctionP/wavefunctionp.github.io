December 11, 2002

Relief for Your Computing Obsession

Anybody who spends a signifigant time behind the computer will agree that often times it can be painful. Many people that spend thier waking hours typing on the keyboard have found that they start losing feeling in their hands and start experiencing pains away from the computer. Common tasks like opening a door become a dreaded affair because of the painful effect. Carpal Tunnet Syndrome (CTS) has gotten plenty of meadia attention. Still many don't know why they are experiencing pain, although there are many effective and inexpensive treatments that could aleviate most of the symptoms. Ars has a review up about the IMAK Smart Glove, a product to help protect your wrist from further injury while allowing them to heal.

Many people have this problem, but a few have a different problem called Cubital Tunnel Syndrom. The main difference in symptoms is that the numbness primarily occurs on the ring and pinky finger. It is caused by inflamation on a nerve that travels through the elbow as apposed to the wrist. The most effective treatment is simple to not rest your elbow on anything to relieve the pressure and inflamation. Along with freaquent breaks, many see full recovery.

I can testify to it simply because I have this problem. When at it's worst I could hardly use my right hand for anything, it was simply to weak and the pain was unbearable. I had tried all the techniques for relief for CTS, but they weren't effective. I finally went to the doctor and he informed me of CuTS. It took me a while to get used to not resting my elbow as I typed, but after a while I became more aware of my elbow position. I also took frequent breaks away from the keyboard to straighten my arm. After about 3 weeks my pain went away and, after about 3 months, I recieved full feeling back to my hand. There is relief to these problems, but it requires a little change to your routine. The change was well worth it though. Don't forget to talk to your doctor about it.

December 01, 2002

DoE and Artifcial sight team up

The Department of Energy has commited to contibuting $3 million over 3 years to artificial retina research.

An artificial retina works by implanting an electrode array on the back of the eye to stimulate the nerves that carry the signals the brain where it is processed to produce the images we see everyday. This has been done before in limited form, usually consisting only a few (~16) electrodes, to allow a patient with retinal disease to see the presence or absence of light. Plans to use more (~1000) electrodes will allow a person to see images; albiet in a very limited form.

I can't wait to get an enhancment implant. I would love to be able to see in the dark or see light out of the visual spectrum. I know that to that would require a way to represent the extra information (other wavelengths outside the visible), as our brains don't readily process those spectrums, but I'm sure someone will figure it out.

I suppose it'll be a while before the technology is refined enough to allow for aesthetic motivations, but it'll come one day. I'll be first in line, of course along with my memory implant too.

By the way, this site is designed to be viewed at 1024X786 resolution AT LEAST! I use 1280X1024, kinda weird but it's the default resolution on my LCD. So, if your not looking at this site by those stardards, your doing yourself a grave misjustice. I saw my site at 800X600 and it looks just horrible, text all grainy and otherwise nasty looking. Frankly, I don't see how people use a computer at such low res. I'll go nuts using it and trying to work on a computer. You can't read anything at that resolution. Well you can, but it look horrendous and you'll end up scrolling for days just to read one article in most websites.

For those of us less fortunate, and don't know what I'm talking about or don't know how to change their resolution. It's really easy on a Windows machine. Right click on the desktop, yeah, the one that probably has a horrible picture as a background because you haven't changed the windows default

Go to "Settings", and there is a slider on the lower left. Moving it to the left makes the resolution change lower, (mental note where is now, before you change it) and moving it right makes the resolution higher.

Most machines you can only change it to safe resolutions, so don't worry about messing things up. Notice the number listed below the slider, that's the resolution you currently have selected. Bump the slider up a couple of notches 'till you get at least to 1024 (FYI, The first number is the how many dots are displayed horizonally (left to right) and the second is the number vertically (Top to bottom). You can get an idea of what the screen will look like after you change it, by looking at the little picture.

If you can, change the color depth to 32 with the dropdown window to the right (the one with the line of colors below it), it'll let you see more colors (than if your set to 16 or 24). If you can'just use the highest one you can. Then click apply, it may ask you if you want to test the settings first, click yes.

Yeah, the screen will go blank or do some kind of test screen, you didn't mess any thing up. The new settings should now be applied, and your screen will look much different (read: better). If you see anything messed up or just got a blank screen the whole time. You set the settings to high and it'll either ask you if you want to change back or keep the new settings or it'll do it automatically.

If it looks good, keep 'em, if not click no. Play around with the settings untill you find the ones you like, just remember to click "apply", not "ok", so you can test your settings first. Now enjoy your new, persective on computing. It should now be a LOT easier to read web pages. and other things of that nature.
